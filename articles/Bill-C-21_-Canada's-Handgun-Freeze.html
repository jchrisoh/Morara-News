Bill C-21: Canada's Handgun Freeze
By Owen Hu | Published Jun 5, 2022 8:10 p.m. PST
Following several high-profile deadly shootings in the US, including the Robb Elementary School shooting in Uvalde, Texas, and the Buffalo supermarket shooting just a few days prior, Canadian Prime Minister Justin Trudeau has proposed Bill C-21, which would effectively freeze the buying of handguns for Canadians.

Trudeau’s proposed legislation would amend the Firearms Act to prevent any further trade of handguns across the country; however, Bill C-21 would not revoke handgun ownership from those currently with them. Moreover, the legislation does not make handguns illegal—those with a valid firearms license, the acquisition of which requires the passing of a safety course, are still able to hold on to their guns. What the bill does do is freeze any transfer of handguns, including buying and selling, and allow for the revoking of high-risk people’s licenses.

These developments come after a 71% increase in the number of registered handguns in Canada in the past decade. With over 12 million firearms belonging to civilians, which calculates to around 34.7 handguns per 100 Canadians, both gun ownership and rates of gun violence in Canada are steadily increasing. However, these recent gains pale in comparison to ownership rates in the United States, where there are over 120 handguns per 100 Americans.

The Canadian government plans for the bill to be fully implemented by the fall of 2022, with Trudeau wishing to enact the bill as soon as possible with the help of opposition parties. However, such has not been easy: critics of the bill, mainly from gun rights activists, have denounced the intentions and expected results of the bill. The Canadian Coalition for Firearms Rights has labeled the Bill C-21 “dangerous liberal propaganda” on Twitter, claiming the effort will “do nothing to reduce violent crime in Canada”. Moreover, opponents argue that a better step to take would be to focus on the principal reason for the rise in gun violence by addressing criminal gangs’ illegal smuggling of arms and alleviating poverty in vulnerable regions of the country.

Unanticipated consequences of the bill include a surge in gun demand following its announcement. In British Columbia, long lines due to high demand led to several stores running out of stock as interested customers scrambled to buy a handgun before the bill comes into force. Bill C-21 has also drawn the attention of women’s groups, who argue that part of the bill’s red flag protocols, which allow anyone to bring a gun owner to court if they believe the suspect would be a threat, would unreasonably shift the responsibility of safety onto citizens and increase the risk of domestic violence victims, particularly women, to further abuse and threats.

Nonetheless, Canada’s efforts to act in the face of tragedy is an important step; whether or not it is in the right direction is highly contested by supporters and opponents alike. Regardless of its effects, Bill C-21 represents Canada's aspirations to do and be better, and it is in the interests of Canadians for it to help us all.
