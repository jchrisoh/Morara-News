University Applications: What's the Difference Between Early Decision, Early Action, and Regular Decision?
By Holly Li | Published Aug 31, 2021 11:38 a.m. PST
Canadian Universities
Early Acceptance:
Except for the University of Toronto, most Canadian Universities offer early acceptance. If students submit their application early, universities might look at students’ GPA from grade 11 and offer early acceptance. Different universities and different programs have different policies regarding early acceptance. Usually, the early acceptance is given out in December or January, which are several months ahead of the regular acceptance date.

U.S. Universities
Early Decision:
Early Decision (ED) allows students to submit their application early and receive a result well ahead of the regular response. However, one student can only apply for one university during early decision, and it is binding to attend if the student gets accepted.

The popular belief is that Early Decision can increase a student’s chance of being admitted to their dream school. While this belief is somewhat true, colleges also look for students who fit the school’s unique features (such as school culture, diversity, academic interests, etc.) of the school. Therefore, spending more time researching the colleges and completing the supplemental questions with care and time might increase a student’s chance of being admitted in Early Decision.

On the other hand, Early Decision has a down side: students are not able to know their financial aid package before they get accepted nor compare the financial aid packages offered by different schools. Because the admission of Early Decision is binding, students must attend the university they applied for in Early Decision even if the financial package is not in their interest. This might be a disadvantage to students who need substantial financial aid.

Early Action:
Early Action (EA) is similar to Early Decision except for the binding agreement of attendance. This means that students can apply for multiple universities during Early Action, and it is not mandatory to attend the university if they get accepted.

The advantage of Early Action is the flexibility of financial aid packages: students are able to compare the financial aid packages provided by multiple different schools before making their decision.

Regular Decision:
The deadline of a regular decision is usually the start of January or February. Students will have more time to work on their applications and study for standardized tests if they choose to apply for Regular Decision. However, the competition is usually more intense during Regular Decision.

Deadlines
The deadline of Early Decision is usually November 1st; the deadline of early action is usually November 1st or January 1st, whereas there might be slight variations depending on colleges’ policies. Some schools even offer multiple rounds of early decision and early action. It is important to check the deadlines before applying.
