Canada's Record High Temperature: Lytton, BC
By Owen Hu | Published Jul 1, 2021 3:51 p.m. PST
It is July 5, 1937, in the small Saskatchewan town of Yellow Grass. The thermometer reads a sweltering 45°C (113°F), the highest temperature ever recorded in Canada at that time. This record would go on to cling to its title for the next eight decades, until it was surpassed by a historic heat wave Lytton, BC.

The heat wave that descended upon the Pacific Northwest in late June of 2021 has baffled climatologists and meteorologists alike. With hot air trapped under atmospheric pressure in “dome–like” manner, the temperatures have soared in provinces such as British Columbia and Alberta. A consequence of these recent events is the shattering of an 84 year Canadian record for the highest temperature ever recorded.

The small village of Lytton, BC, located in the interior of the province along the Fraser River, faced a record high temperature of 46.7°C (116°F) on June 27, 2021. Beyond simply raising the bar for the highest temperature, there are several repercussions of this parching heat. From a public health standpoint, these record temperatures are concerning as methods to keep cool, such as air conditioning, are not standardized throughout the province. With temperatures rarely reaching above 30°C (86°F), many British Columbians never had the need to keep air conditioning in their homes. The government has voiced concerns of heatstroke, especially on vulnerable demographic groups that are especially at risk, and has also encouraged citizens to stay hydrated. In response, many municipalities have opened cooling centers in an attempt to minimize the serious health issues that may arise from the heat.

The recent shattering of the record has also prompted discussion on the role of climate change. Global scientists have reached a mutual consensus that the recent uptick in average temperature each summer in BC is playing a significant role in this historic heat wave. Many climatologists even warn that such events may become more and more common in the future, and that this heat dome will not be a one–off thing.

With a not–so–pretty outlook on the future, British Columbians are scrambling to utilize any relief they can find to beat the heat. The future is quite uncertain, but what is certain is that for now, every citizen should drink plenty of water, stay hydrated, and hope for cooler days to come.
