Eileen Gu vs. Zhu Yi: Different Stories of Foreign Athletes
By Enya Fang | Published Feb 20, 2022 5:51 p.m. PST
After the country hosted the Summer Games nearly a decade and a half ago, Team China is risking long-standing geopolitical turmoil to put its best foot forward at the 2022 Beijing Olympics.

The star of the team, 18-year-old freestyle skier Eileen Gu, became the first freeski athlete of any gender to medal at 3 separate events in the same Winter Games: gold in both the halfpipe and big air, and silver in slopestyle. The reaction from netizens? A mixture of pride and disgust.

“I am both Chinese and American.” Gu says in an interview with USA Today. “The U.S. has made me who I am. China has made me who I am, and I am infinitely grateful to both.”

Eileen, who is fluent in Mandarin as a result of spending much of her youth in China, chose to represent her mother’s homeland over her birthplace of San Francisco. In doing so, she hopes she inspires more girls and women in China to compete in winter sports.

“My biggest goal is for some girl to be sitting at home watching freeskiing for the first time and thinking, ‘maybe that could be me someday’,” Gu said.

Despite that, questions persist about her citizenship. The International Olympic Committee requires athletes to have a passport from the country they’re competing for, and China does not typically allow dual citizenship. Gu has not confirmed whether she renounced her American citizenship.

Other critics have questioned whether she has had to make compromises to do business in China. The U.S. State Department has called China’s mistreatment of Uyghur Muslims in Xinjiang “genocide” and led a diplomatic boycott of these Games. Gu rejected the notion that to do business in China has required her to keep quiet on issues of human rights.

The dichotomy is ever-present in China as well. Gu’s unwavering success at Beijing 2022 has hailed her as a national hero, but Chinese American figure skater Zhu Yi who fell during her first two Olympic performances has received a torrent of abuse on Weibo, a popular Chinese social media platform.

Both born in California, Gu and Zhu’s decision to represent the host nation, China, have been used to question the loyalty of Asian Americans. Though the female athletes garnered the most headlines, they’re just 2 of 30 foreign-born athletes on Team China. Nearly half are North American men's hockey players, and all reportedly maintained citizenship.

“People sometimes don’t know what to do with other people when they’re not fitting in a box,” Gu told reporters after the halfpipe qualifying event.

“They say, ‘Is she Chinese? Is she American? Is she a model? Is she a student? Why is she trying to change the world when she’s only 18?’”
