What is the Land Back Movement?
By Lily Nordgren | Published Feb 23, 2021 1:07 p.m. PST
In British Columbia, the Coastal GasLink Pipeline has brought local attention to the phrase "Land Back". Wet'suwet'en land defenders set up blockades and demonstrated to prevent the pipeline from being constructed on their territory. In the November 2020 Globe and Mail article "'Land Back' is more than a slogan for a resurgent Indigenous movement", Kanahus Manuel and Naomi Klein pointed out that "Land Back" has been echoed all over Canada; in Nova Scotia by Mi'kmaq fishermen facing violence from white fishermen, in Ontario by Haudenosaunee peoples fighting a housing development on their territory, and in B.C. against the Coastal GasLink pipeline.

According to a 4Rs Youth Movement article, "Land Back! What do we mean?", written by Ronald Gamblin, "Land Back" has a few different meanings. It defines the physical transfer of land to Indigenous peoples, the protection and reclamation of land, and self-governance for Indigenous nations. "Land Back" fights against the oppression of colonial structures like residential schools, forced relocation, and attempted assimilation. Land negotiations and discussions between Indigenous nations and the Canadian government are often met with criticism. As outlined in a January 2019 Global News article by Maham Abedi, "Band councils, hereditary chiefs - here's what to know about Indigenous governance", Indigenous governance is often structured differently than federal governance. Hereditary chiefs are recognized as community leaders within Indigenous nations. Band councils were created by the Indian Act, and are elected councils that serve to administrate federal funding. The decisions of hereditary chiefs and of band councils will not necessarily align, especially on a topic with as much uncertainty and trauma as unceded land.

The Wet'suwet'en nation and Coastal GasLink pipeline issue highlighted the complexity in governance over Indigenous land. In the March 2020 Global News article by Hina Alam, "Wet'suwet'en pipeline supporters say their message is not being heard", Alam wrote that there were conflicting decisions between hereditary chiefs and band councils. Chief Na'moks of the Tsayu Clan, who spoke to Global News, said that Wet'suwet'en people who support the pipeline should have a "right to express themselves". Chief Na'moks opposes the pipeline, but says that Indigenous peoples have thousands of years of experience governing the land, and decisions about the future must be made together. Some Wet'suwet'en people support the pipeline for economic reasons, and hope that the resources will be beneficial. Differing opinions aside, authority and rights over unceded land legally goes to Indigenous people.

"Land Back" covers many meanings, and will need to be defined differently in specific circumstances. The movement has raised questions of power, of authority, and of decolonization. The answers require a shift in our understanding of land and its ownership.
